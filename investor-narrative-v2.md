# RealFast Investor Narrative v2

> **The Skeptic-Tested Version**

---

## The Pitch (30 seconds)

RealFast is an AI-first IT services company with two revenue engines:

1. **Salesforce** — Accelerated implementations through the SF channel (vertical, distribution built-in)
2. **AI Transformation** — Helping enterprises execute on AI initiatives that actually reach production (horizontal, any industry)

We use AI not just as a tool, but as a **structural redesign of how services are delivered** — smaller teams, fixed bids, faster time-to-value.

---

# The Skeptical VC Analysis

*Below, we present the hardest questions a VC would ask — and our honest answers.*

---

## Objection 1: "You're just a services business. Services don't scale."

### The Skeptic Says:

> *"I've seen a hundred 'AI-powered services' companies. They all hit the same wall: revenue scales linearly with headcount. You're selling hours, not software. Where's the venture-scale outcome?"*

### Our Response:

**We're not denying we're a services business. We're arguing the economics of services are being restructured.**

| Traditional Services | RealFast Model |
|---------------------|----------------|
| 10 people for a $500K project | 3-4 people + AI for same project |
| Margin = Revenue - (Headcount × Cost) | Margin = Revenue - (Fewer Heads × Cost + Platform) |
| Revenue/head is capped | Revenue/head expands with AI leverage |

**The math**:
- If we deliver with 60% fewer people at the same price, our margin expands dramatically
- If we price 20% lower to win more deals, we still have better margins AND higher close rates
- The constraint isn't "can we deliver" — it's "can we sell faster than we deliver"

**The platform angle**:
- Exo Cortex is the platform we use internally — it's not vaporware
- Phase 1: We use it. Phase 2: Clients see dashboards. Phase 3: We license it.
- The platform is funded by services, not by burn

**Why this matters**: We're not asking you to believe we'll build a platform someday. We've already built it. The question is whether the services business generates enough proof to make platform licensing credible.

## Objection 2: "Everyone claims to be AI-first. What's actually different?"

### The Skeptic Says:

> *"Accenture, Deloitte, Infosys, TCS — they all have 'AI practices' now. What makes you different from a Big 4 consultancy that just hired 500 AI engineers?"*

### Our Response:

**The difference is structural, not cosmetic.**

| Big SI "AI Practice" | RealFast |
|---------------------|----------|
| AI as an add-on service line | AI as the operating model |
| Existing delivery processes + AI tools | Delivery rebuilt from first principles around AI |
| Headcount is the product | Outcome is the product |
| T&M billing (hours × rate) | Fixed bids (we take the delivery risk) |
| Knowledge in senior heads | Knowledge in playbooks + agents |

**Why they can't easily pivot**:
- Large SIs have thousands of people whose jobs depend on the old model
- Their business model rewards headcount growth, not efficiency
- Their incentives are misaligned — more efficient delivery = less revenue

**Our unfair advantage**:
- We're small enough to rebuild around AI without legacy drag
- We're engineering-led, not sales-led
- We've already built the platform (Exo Cortex) that encodes our AI-native delivery

**The honest answer**: Big SIs will eventually figure this out. Our bet is that we can build enough proof points and platform moat before they do. We're not trying to out-resource them — we're trying to out-execute them in a specific wedge (Salesforce) and expand from there.

## Objection 3: "Two buckets means no focus. Pick one."

### The Skeptic Says:

> *"You're either a Salesforce shop or an AI transformation company. Which is it? I see two different businesses here, and early-stage companies die from lack of focus."*

### Our Response:

**They're not two businesses. They're two distribution strategies for the same capability.**

| Dimension | Salesforce (Bucket 1) | AI Transformation (Bucket 2) |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| **What we do** | AI-accelerated delivery | AI-accelerated delivery |
| **How we deliver** | Same team, same platform | Same team, same platform |
| **Distribution** | SF channel (pre-built) | Direct sales (we build) |
| **Why it exists** | Proof points, cash flow | TAM expansion |

**The strategic logic**:
1. **Salesforce is our beachhead** — distribution is built-in, deals are flowing, we don't have to build demand
2. **AI Transformation is our expansion** — once we have proof points, we can sell horizontally
3. **They share the same delivery model** — Automate/Augment/Connect framework applies to both

**The honest answer**: If we had to pick one, we'd pick Salesforce today because the distribution is free. But AI Transformation is where the larger TAM is. We're not running two businesses — we're running one business with two go-to-market motions.

## Objection 4: "Fixed bids are risky. You're taking all the delivery risk."

### The Skeptic Says:

> *"If you commit to fixed prices and projects go sideways, you eat the loss. T&M exists for a reason — it protects the services provider. Why would you take on that risk?"*

### Our Response:

**Fixed bids are only risky if you can't estimate accurately. Our platform makes us confident in our estimates.**

| Traditional Estimation | RealFast Estimation |
|-----------------------|---------------------|
| Senior consultant's gut feel | Historical data from Manazer |
| Padded for uncertainty | Calibrated from actual delivery times |
| High variance | Confidence scoring (we know when we don't know) |

**Why we can do this**:
- Exo Cortex tracks every project's actual hours vs. estimated
- We have empirical data on how long things take, not guesses
- Our temporal clustering algorithm infers work time from artifacts — no manual tracking bias
- When confidence is low, we don't bid fixed — we're honest about uncertainty

**The strategic value**:
- Clients love fixed bids — it de-risks their budget
- We win more deals because clients prefer outcome certainty
- Higher close rates offset the occasional overrun

**The honest answer**: We have had projects go over. But because we track everything, we learn from each one. The data compounds. Traditional SIs can't do fixed bids at scale because they don't have the data foundation we do.

## Objection 5: "Where's the platform revenue? You're still just selling hours."

### The Skeptic Says:

> *"You say you're building a platform, but all your revenue is services. When does the platform actually generate revenue? This sounds like 'we'll be a SaaS company someday' — but you're not."*

### Our Response:

**Fair. Platform revenue is Phase 3. We're in Phase 1.**

| Phase | Focus | Revenue Model |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| **Phase 1 (Now)** | Internal use | Services revenue, platform cost |
| **Phase 2 (Near-term)** | Client dashboards | Services revenue, client stickiness |
| **Phase 3 (Medium-term)** | Platform licensing | Platform revenue from other SIs |

**Why this sequence makes sense**:
- We're not burning capital to build a platform no one uses
- We're our own first customer — the platform has to work for us
- Every client engagement improves the platform
- By the time we license, we'll have years of usage data and iteration

**The honest answer**: If you're looking for platform revenue today, we're not the right investment. If you believe that AI will restructure services delivery and that the winning platform will come from a practitioner (not a pure software company), then we're building the right thing in the right sequence.

## Objection 6: "$100K/quarter is not venture-scale."

### The Skeptic Says:

> *"You're targeting $100K revenue from Singapore SF channel in a quarter. That's 20 deals at $5K average. This is a lifestyle business, not a venture-scale opportunity."*

### Our Response:

**$100K is our Singapore SF channel target — not our total addressable opportunity.**

**Let's be precise about the math**:
- Singapore SF channel (Q1 2025): $100K target, 20 deals
- This is ONE geography, ONE channel, ONE quarter
- Average deal size: ~$5K-25K (varies by play)

**The expansion path**:

| Expansion Vector | Impact |
|-----------------|--------|
| Geography | Singapore → ASEAN → APAC → Global |
| Channel | SMB → Mid-Market → Enterprise |
| Product | SF implementations → Agentforce specialty → Full AI transformation |
| Model | Services → Services + Platform licensing |

**The venture case**:
- Global IT services market: $1.2T+
- If AI restructures 10% of that market, $120B in play
- If we capture 0.1% of that restructured market, that's $120M
- We don't need to win the whole market — we need to win a defensible wedge

**The honest answer**: We're not raising to stay small. We're raising to prove the model in Singapore, then replicate across geographies. The Singapore target is a proof point, not a ceiling.

## Objection 7: "You're dependent on Salesforce. What if they change partner economics?"

### The Skeptic Says:

> *"Your Bucket 1 is entirely dependent on Salesforce's channel. What if they squeeze partner margins? What if Agentforce flops? You have platform risk."*

### Our Response:

**Salesforce is our beachhead, not our entire strategy.**

**Why SF is low-risk as a starting point**:
- SF has a vested interest in partner success — partners implement their software
- Agentforce is a strategic priority — SF is pushing AI hard
- We're aligned with SF's incentives: more successful implementations = more SF revenue

**What happens if SF channel changes**:
- Bucket 2 (AI Transformation) is platform-agnostic
- The skills we build on SF (AI-accelerated delivery) transfer to other platforms
- Exo Cortex works for any software delivery, not just Salesforce

**The honest answer**: Yes, we have concentration risk today. That's why Bucket 2 exists — it's our diversification strategy. The goal is to use SF to build proof points, then expand horizontally where we control our own distribution.

## Objection 9: "What stops Accenture from copying this?"

### The Skeptic Says:

> *"If this model works, what stops a Big 4 consultancy from throwing $100M at it and copying everything you've built?"*

### Our Response:

**They can try. Here's why it's hard:**

| Barrier | Why It Protects Us |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Incentive misalignment** | Big SIs profit from headcount; efficiency hurts their model |
| **Legacy process debt** | They have thousands of people trained on old methods |
| **Speed of iteration** | We ship weekly; they ship quarterly (at best) |
| **Platform head start** | Exo Cortex has years of development; they'd start from zero |
| **Data moat** | Every project improves our models; they don't have the data |

**The honest answer**: Accenture could absolutely build this if they wanted to. The question is whether they will, and whether they'll move fast enough. Large organizations are structurally bad at disrupting themselves. We're betting on their inertia.

**The real moat**: It's not the technology — it's the compounding data and the willingness to cannibalize the old model. We don't have an old model to protect.
