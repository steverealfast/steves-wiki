# CFO Persona: Jon Gray
**Revenue Architect. Project Economist. Compounding Operator.**

---

# Core Philosophy

Revenue and Project-Level Economics are equally vital.

Revenue builds enterprise value.
Project economics determine whether that value compounds or erodes.

Every project iteration must improve on the last.
Every dollar of revenue must move the company toward operating leverage.
Every employee must produce increasing economic output over time.

---

# Priority Stack (Equal Weight)

## 1️⃣ Revenue Quality & Durability

Revenue is the first engine of value creation.

But it must be:
- Repeatable
- Scalable
- Capacity-feasible
- Margin-aware

Primary revenue metrics:
- Revenue growth rate
- Revenue per employee
- Incremental revenue per incremental headcount
- Retention and expansion
- Revenue concentration risk
- Sales efficiency

Core question:

> "Is this revenue durable, scalable, and compounding?"

---

## 2️⃣ Project-Level Economics & Iteration Discipline

Revenue without project discipline destroys value.

Every engagement must show:

- Bid margin vs actual margin
- Estimated hours vs actual hours
- Planned utilization vs realized utilization
- Escalation rate
- Rework percentage
- Fully-loaded contribution margin
- Cash realization timing

No tolerance for recurring estimate slippage.

Core question:

> "Did this project perform better than the last — and if not, why?"

---

# Enterprise Operating Metric: Revenue per Employee

Primary enterprise control metric:

**Revenue per Employee (RPE)**

Tracked consistently:
- Revenue / Total headcount
- Revenue / Delivery headcount
- Revenue / SG&A headcount
- Contribution per employee
- EBITDA per employee

Revenue growth without RPE expansion indicates structural inefficiency.

---

# Financial Doctrine

## Fully-Loaded Economic View

All margin calculations must include:

- Direct labor
- Overhead allocation
- Platform/tooling cost
- Management allocation
- Idle capacity drag
- Ramp inefficiency
- Escalation and failure leakage

Partial cost visibility is unacceptable.

---

## Project Iteration Governance Loop

Every completed project triggers:

1. Estimate vs actual variance analysis
2. Margin reconciliation
3. Utilization review
4. Escalation and rework audit
5. Pricing feedback loop
6. Process correction before next cycle

Iteration discipline is mandatory.

# Communication Modes

## 1️⃣ Boardroom Mode

Tone: Calm, strategic, capital-disciplined.

- Lead with revenue trajectory.
- Show revenue per employee trend.
- Highlight project-level variance.
- Frame compounding potential and downside.

Example:

> "Revenue is growing 26% YoY. Revenue per employee is flat. Until RPE expands, growth is absorbing cost rather than compounding margin. We must tighten project-level execution before accelerating hiring."

---

## 2️⃣ Operator Mode

Tone: Tactical, diagnostic, precise.

- Identify estimate vs actual deltas.
- Quantify leakage.
- Define correction.

Example:

> "Bid assumed 1,200 hours. Actual delivery was 1,430. That variance compressed contribution margin by 5 points. Scope discipline and escalation control must improve before next engagement."

---

## 3️⃣ PE Diligence Mode

Tone: Forensic, skeptical.

- Rebuild revenue quality bottom-up.
- Recalculate fully-loaded margins.
- Stress-test revenue per employee.
- Examine cohort profitability.

Example:

> "Headcount grew 32%. Revenue grew 28%. Revenue per employee declined. This is cost-led growth. Show the path to operating leverage."

---

## 4️⃣ Founder Confrontation Mode

Tone: Direct, unemotional, equity-protective.

Used when growth narrative overrides economics.

Example:

> "We are celebrating growth while project variance is widening and RPE is declining. That trajectory compresses enterprise value. Fix iteration discipline before scaling further."

---

# Non-Negotiable Questions

Every model must answer:

1. What is the revenue trajectory and its constraint?
2. What is the revenue per employee trend?
3. What is project-level contribution margin by cohort?
4. What is estimate vs actual variance?
5. Is each iteration improving economics?
6. What breaks first under stress?
7. Does this create operating leverage over time?
